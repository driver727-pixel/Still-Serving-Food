This food blog details my personal experience working within the food industry, specifically my time spent at a food bank Warehouse (Community Action Coalition of South Central Wisconsin) delivering pizzas for 15 years for Glass Nickel Pizza and also took on the role of managing the Willy Street Co-op Deli. Over a period of more than 20 years, through these various work experiences, what I came to realize and what I learned fundamentally is that, more often than not, there exists a significant disconnect. This disconnect occurs between the excess food that is generated and comes from various sources like bakeries, restaurants, and local farmers, and the final recipients who desperately need it, which are the food pantries and the food banks. This lack of connection is a widespread issue. A lot of the time, those crucial connections are typically made in a very manual, one-by-one manner, requiring human-to-human interaction. This often involves a simple phone call or a direct text message. Over and Over. Every week during the pantries existence. It is only in very rare, exceptional cases that these entities can achieve a level of automation for large, routine, and regular shipments. Even when automation occurs, it usually relies on basic technology like simple spreadsheets or sending out emails. Crucially, the practical application of technology to solve this systemic problem essentially stops right there. Recognizing this significant gap and utilizing my own skills in software development and my computer tech savvy expertise, I took the initiative to build a dedicated web application. I named this web app madfoodloop.com. This particular website is owned and operated by my Wisconsin-based limited liability company, which is called SP Digital LLC. The primary purpose of this website is to efficiently connect those two main, essential parties in the food distribution chain. These two critical parties are the producers who have the excess food supplies and the pantries that are in need of that accessible surplus food. The web application is designed to accomplish this vital connection in a couple of different ways. One of the methods is half  automated, providing a brief weekly text questionnaire process. The other method focuses on forming simple, single-click connections directly between the producer and the pantry. While I was in the process of building this web application, I quickly came across a major, unavoidable hurdle that presented a real challenge. Connecting the two parties virtually within the digital environment of a website was one part of the problem, and I had solved that. However, the physical reality of actually getting the excess food physically moved from Point A to Point B requires logistical support. In an often significant number of cases, this requires volunteer drivers to handle the transfer. The challenge is that neither the restaurant producing the food nor the food pantry receiving it has the necessary staff, nor the spare time, nor the labor dollars to financially spend on facilitating these crucial transfers. Therefore, while researching potential solutions for this logistical gap in the idea, I discovered a specific and valuable concept. This concept is known as "volunteer hours". This system operates where a major company will agree to pay an employee their regular wage to perform community service duties during what would normally be their regular work day. Importantly, this service is performed instead of them doing their regular work tasks. It is structured so that this community service time still officially counts as time worked towards the employer's operational requirements. For example, a paid employee can dedicate a period of 2 hours to go and deliver food donations. This happens when they are experiencing a slow period at their primary day job. Crucially, this time delivering food donations counts towards their total work time that pay period. I have personally heard of several large companies in the Madison area that already participate in and utilize this volunteer hours concept, including Google, ZenDesk, Epic, and DoorDash. Consequently, I proceeded to integrate this functionality directly into the web application. The app now includes this dedicated functionality. There is a certain legitimacy and established process to this feature. The entire website can be run and operated very efficiently. It is also fully optimized in its operation and can be run for a remarkably cheap cost. The total monthly cost is for less than approximately $20 a month. So, let's consider a scenario where a corporate sponsor, perhaps a company based in Madison, wants to provide sponsorship for this initiative. They would sponsor the site for the reasonable price of $20 a month. In return for this contribution, they will be recognized and become a "hero sponsor". Their official name and corporate logo will then be prominently displayed and appear on all of the receipts that are generated for tax write-offs. All of the involved parties receive these receipts. Specifically, when a "food Loop" is successfully closed, three parties—the pantry receiving the food, the producer supplying the food, and the volunteer driver completing the delivery—all automatically receive a PDF document. This PDF has a logo on it. The specific logo and name featured on the PDF will be that of the dedicated hero sponsor for that particular month. Boom. Easy. Additionally, the application will also accept financial support from any private individuals who wish to contribute, which can be done through the readily available support button on the website.
